# cubic_4folds
Tables of cubic fourfolds and their geometric invariants, motivated by Manin's conjecture and the circle method.

The fewnomials data was generated by SageMath through CoCalc, using code written by Gemini 3 Pro.
For each non-conical, reduced, irreducible cubic fourfold X in P^5_Q defined by a sum of k pairwise distinct primitive monomials, the code computes the dimension and degree of the singular subscheme of X, as well as the degree of the dual variety of X, and whether the dual variety of X is a hypersurface or not.
The data for k = 2, 3, and 4 is available [here](https://github.com/wangyangvictor/cubic_4folds/blob/main/fewnomials/cubic_4fold_k2_data.csv), [here](https://github.com/wangyangvictor/cubic_4folds/blob/main/fewnomials/cubic_4fold_k3_data.csv), and [here](https://github.com/wangyangvictor/cubic_4folds/blob/main/fewnomials/cubic_4fold_k4_data.csv), respectively.
For any given X, the data can be checked on [Magma](https://magma.maths.usyd.edu.au/calc/), for example using the script [here](https://github.com/wangyangvictor/cubic_4folds/blob/main/magma/magma_dual_alt.txt).

It would be interesting to compute further information about these varieties X, such as (1) whether their generic hyperplane sections satisfy "better than square-root cancellation" as in the work of Browning--Munshi--Wang, or (2) if not, what kind of Hasse--Weil L-functions arise, in the simplest possible form.
Moreover, one might want to know (3) the identity component of the automorphism group of X.
As a bonus, it might be good to know (4) the set of planes contained in X,
and (5) how to interpret point-counting on X(Q) in terms of the Manin--Peyre conjecture, if possible,
or (6) in terms of more general conjectures of Batyrev--Tschinkel, Lehmann--Sengupta--Tanimoto, et al.

It is plausible that a mixture of human expertise, computer algebra, and AI/Lean assistance could be useful for many of the tasks listed above.
